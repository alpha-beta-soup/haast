Dr. Johann Franz Julius von Haast

Passing from Bonn to Haast's Pass

A celebration of a New Zealand geographical pioneer, for FOSS4G 2016 (Bonn, Germany)

  • 1 May 1822 Born in Bonn, Germany, the son of Anna Eva Theodora Ruth and her husband Mathias Haast, a merchant and sometime burgomaster (magistrate or mayor) of Bonn.
  • 1820s-1830s Educated at grammar schools in Bonn and Cologne.
  • 1840s Studied geology at the Rheinische Friedrich-Wilhelms-Universität in Bonn, but did not graduate.
  • 1840s After travelling throughout Europe, Haast settles in Frankfurt, and works in the trade of books and also mineral samples.
  • 26 October 1846 Married Antonia Johanna Caroline Schmitt in Frankfurt. Ultimately, the marriage was not happy.
  • 1848 With his wife, Haast had a son, Matthias Robert.
  • 1 May 1858 Accepted a proposal from the English shipowners, Willis, Gann and Co., to travel to New Zealand and to report on the prospects for German emigration. Haast was fluent in English. Haast's eventual report was not favourable for the prospects of emigration, citing the young nation's financial difficulties, and hostilities between immigrants and Māori.
  • September 1858—21 December 1858 Travelled from London to Auckland, New Zealand on the Evening Star.
  • 22 December 1858 Ferdinand Hochstetter (a geologist from Esslingen, Württemberg) arrives in Auckland on the Australian frigate Novara. Haast meets hims this very day, at the home of another German emigrant, and the two become lifelong friends.
  • 1859 Haast and Hochstetter work together to do a number of geological expeditions in the Auckland and Nelson areas. Haast accompanies Hochstetter in investigating a coal field in Drury, where he also meets the explorer and surveror Charles Heaphy. They journey along the Waikato River, interacting with many Māori before retuning to Auckland in January 1859. Soon after, the party would explore much more of the Waikato region, mostly untraversed by Pākehā, making its own maps.
  • June 1859 Hochstetter and Haast go to the Coromandel to investigate gold fields.
  • July 1859 Hochstetter and Haast sail to Nelson. during this time, Haast performs several digs for moa bones, extracting several near-complete skeletons. At Shakespeare Bay, near the present-day site of the town of Picton, Haast correctly predicts the presence of gold based upon his inspection of the local geology.
  • October 1859 Antonia Haast dies.
  • Late 1859 Hochstetter departs from New Zealand; Haast is requested by the Nelson provincial government to spend eight months exploring the western parts of the province, a proposal to which he agrees.
  • December 1859 Haast proposes that the Nelson geological survey be extended into Canterbury.
  • 1860 Haast's report on the Nelson province revealed the extent of the Grey River coalfields (initially discovered but underestimated by Thomas Brunner), and found the Coalbrookdale (Denniston) seam in the Buller River, which will significantly impact the development of the town of Westport. He also reported traces of gold found in West Coast rivers.
  • November 1860 Haast is requested by the Canterbury Superintendent William Moorhouse to examine the site of a proposed rail tunnel between Lyttleton and the Canterbury Plains, which a contracter had abandoned after encountering hard rock. Haast's report was favourable and a new tunnel was completed.
  • February 1861 Haast assumes the position of provincial geologist to Canterbury, and from this time is associated with the development of both science and art in Canterbury. Haast held this position until 1869.
  • 18 February 1861 Haast becomes a British subject.
  • 1861 Haast begins a museum based on his own collection, modestly housed in the Canterbury council buildings.
  • 1861—1873 Haast explores and maps the Canterbury region (which included Westland at this time), assessing mineral potential and making many observations.
    • Made expeditions up all of the major Canterbury rivers to their sources. His alpine explorations yielded much new topographical, botanical, and geological information. In time he will collect many plans unknown to science, and many genera and species are named after him. Excavations at historical Māori sites conducted under his supervision led to important contributions to ethnography.
    • Explored the headwaters of the Rangitata River.
    • Made the first systematic examination of the area surrounding Mt Cook, the tallest mountain in New Zealand.
    • 1861 Discovers a coal seam in Kowai.
    • 1863 Crossed what came to be known as the Haast Pass in Haast's honour. This remains one of only three road passes (and one tunnel) of the Main Divide in the South Island of New Zealand. It is the lowest of the three passes, and is now a State Highway (6).
    • 1865 Haast correctly predicts the existence of artesian water beneath the Canterbury Plains gravel.
    • 1865 Named the Franz Josef Glacier after Emperor Franz Joseph I of Austria
  • July 1862 Haast founds the Philosophical Institute of Canterbury. Haast's enthusiam for science and art is inspiration to many. His voice and skill as a violist (he played under Felix Mandelssohn's baton in Düsseldorf) helped enrich the early musical scene of Christchurch.
  • 1862 Haast recieves an honourary degree of Doctor of Philosophy from the University of Tübingen.
  • 25 June 1863 Marries Mary Dobson at St. Mary's Church, Heathcote, Christchurch. They will go on to have five children.
  • 1865 The discovery of a well-preserved store of moa remains in a swamp in Glenmark (Waipara) excites Haast, who boosted the reputation of his museum by sending the remains overseas in exchange for valuable additions to the Canterbury museum. Haast's later papers on the moa and other New Zealand discoveries were read before the Geological and Zoological societies of London and the Royal Geographical Society.
  • 1867 Haast is elected a fellow of the Royal Society.
  • 1868 The expansion of Haast's museum led to the design of a new building by B. W. Beaumont, to which Haast is apppointed director.
  • 1870Haast's new science museum (the Canterbury Museum) is opened, with himself appointed as curator. An ordinance establishes public library and school of technical science in association with the museum; Haast objected to the presence of the school.
  • 1871 Haast is among those who established the Canterbury Collegiate Union, which promoted the establishment of Canterbury College (University of Canterbury) and its affiliation with the University of New Zealand. Haast taught geology and paleotologgy within the college.
  • 1871 Haast's eagle is described for the first time by Julius Haast, from remains found by F. Fuller in a former marsh. Its scientific name, Harpagornis moorei is given by Haast after the name of the owner of the Glenmark Estate, George Henry Moore, on whose property the bones of the first bird were found. Haast's eagle is believed to have become extinct around 1400.
  • 1875 Haast is conferred a hereditary knighthood by the emperor of Austria. This entitles him to the style himself "von Haast".
  • 1876 von Haast becomes the first professor of geology at Canterury College, and held this position until his death.
  • 1879 Publishes Geology of the provinces of Canterbury and Westland, New Zealand. This book summarised his work to date, and includs hand-drawn maps and illustrations, often drawn by his own hand.
  • 1879—1887 von Haast serves as a member of the senate of the University of New Zealand.
  • 1883 Becomes a Companion to the Order of St Michael and St George (CMG)
  • 1884 von Haast is awarded the Patron's Gold Medal of the Royal Geographical Society for his explorations in New Zealand.
  • 1886 Awarded an honourary degree of Doctor of Science from Cambridge University.
  • 1886 von Haast visits Europe as a New Zealand comissioner for the Colonial and Indian Exhibition in London.
  • 1886 Becomes a Knight Commander of the Order of St Michael and St George (KCMG).
  • 16 August 1887 von Haast dies in Christchurch, New Zealand. He is buried in the grounds of the former Holy Trinity Church in Stanmore Road, Christchurch. The church was devastated in the 2011 Canterbury earthquake, and has been demolished. His grave can still be visited near the location of the former church.

Criticisms of Haast

Haast was ambitious, meticulous, and seemingly obsessed with external recognition for his work: actively seeking personal honours in New Zealand and Europe. His reports were voluminous and extremely detailed—adjectives which were used in a disparaging light.

Selected legacy

  • Haast's Pass, New Zealand .
  • Haast, New Zealand , 2006 population of 297 people, spread in three communities: the Haast township, Haast Junction, and Haast Beach.
  • The Haast River , most of which is within the Te Wahipounamu World Heritage site. A road bridge over the river is the longest one-lane road bride in New Zealand, and the seventh-longest bridge in New Zealand.
  • The schist found in New Zealand is called "Haast schist" as a tribute to von Haast's contributions to the nation's geology. The schist is found on the eastern side of the Alpine Fault in the South Island.
  • The Haast brown kiwi, Apteryx haastii, the rarest subspecies of the South Island brown kiwi (only about 250 birds live), bears von Haast's name, and is only found near the town of Haast.
  • Haast's eagle bears his name. It is an extinct species of eagle once extant in the South Island of New Zealand, and was the largest species of eagle known to have existed. It was even larger than the largest living vultures.

About

FOSS4G 2016 will be held in Bonn, Germany, in August. This annual has been held almost every year since 2004. It brings together many of those interested in free and open source software (FOSS) for geographic information systems (GIS). As Haast was born and raised in Bonn, I decided to make this meagre tribute to a man who such an influence on the development of science and in particular geography in New Zealand, my home.
